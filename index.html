A Comprehensive Guide to Using AI
in IB Coursework

Navigating the ethical use of AI tools while maintaining academic integrity

1. Introduction: What Is AI and Why Is It Relevant?

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is a branch of computer science dedicated to creating systems that can simulate human cognitive functions—such as problem-solving, learning, and language understanding. As the "Artificial Intelligence Policy" states, "AI is a branch of computer science focused on creating machines that simulate human intelligence" (Artificial Intelligence Policy, p. 2). Within the IB context, AI tools have rapidly evolved to become integral research assistants, enabling students to generate ideas, refine their writing, and access large volumes of information.

1.1 Defining Generative AI

Generative AI is a subset of AI that creates content—be it text, images, or music—based on patterns learned from vast amounts of training data. For example, ChatGPT, Google Gemini, Claude, Perplexity, Grok, and Deep Research tools are all designed to respond to prompts with humanlike dialogue and creative outputs. As noted in the "Artificial Intelligence Questions and Concerns" document, these tools "respond to a request with an answer that creates a humanlike conversational dialogue" (Artificial Intelligence Questions and Concerns, p. 1). Their ability to generate extended, detailed text or even images makes them valuable for brainstorming and initial research stages.

1.2 Why AI Matters in IB Education

The International Baccalaureate (IB) emphasises the development of independent, critical thinkers who produce authentic and original work. AI can serve as an aid in this learning process, helping students generate ideas and explore multiple viewpoints while still requiring them to synthesise, critique, and validate information on their own. However, the IB also stresses that work produced must be a true reflection of the student's own knowledge and learning ("IB Academic Integrity Policy", p. 3). The challenge for students is to use AI as a tool—much like a calculator or a reference book—without letting it replace their own intellectual engagement.

2. Exploring AI Models and Tools

2.1 Key AI Models

Several AI models are available today, each with its own strengths and weaknesses:

ChatGPT (OpenAI)
Well-known for generating detailed, conversational text. Excellent for long-form content, idea generation, and preliminary drafts.
Google Gemini
A next-generation model designed to improve conversational depth and handle more complex queries.
Claude (Anthropic)
Emphasises ethical output and transparency, making it a solid choice for educational contexts.
Perplexity & Grok
Aimed at providing concise, research-based answers for quick fact-checking or gathering alternative perspectives.
Deep Research Tools
Specialised AI tools that allow for iterative follow-up queries to refine ideas and gain clarity on complex topics.

Each of these tools works on the principle of deep learning, where models are trained on massive datasets and learn to predict the next most likely word or sequence. They continuously update their responses based on input but are also prone to limitations such as biases, inaccuracies, and "hallucinations" (i.e. plausible-sounding yet incorrect output) (Artificial Intelligence Policy, p. 2).

2.2 Choosing the Right Tool

A student might choose ChatGPT for generating extended drafts or exploring thematic ideas, whereas Google Gemini might be preferred when up-to-date information is required. Claude can be beneficial for tasks requiring ethical scrutiny and transparent output, while Perplexity or Grok may be used for short, factual queries. The decision often depends on the assignment's requirements and the student's personal learning style.

3. Best Practices for Using AI in IB Coursework

3.1 AI as a Research Assistant, Not an Author

The IB encourages students to utilise AI as a means of brainstorming, fact-checking, and idea refinement—but not as a substitute for independent thought or to generate complete essays. As stated in "Artificial Intelligence Questions and Concerns," "AI should be used as part of research, not used to produce the work for the student" (Artificial Intelligence Questions and Concerns, p. 3).

3.1.1 Brainstorming and Idea Generation

Students can ask open-ended questions to generate ideas or structure for their essays. For example:

  • "What are the potential biases in the research on climate change?"
  • "Can you suggest some historical case studies that illustrate the impact of technology on society?"

These follow-up questions help refine the student's own understanding and encourage critical evaluation. Importantly, if AI-generated ideas significantly shape the final work, these should be acknowledged (see Section 4).

3.1.2 Prompt Engineering

A key skill is prompt engineering—the process of crafting effective prompts to ensure AI provides useful, accurate responses. For instance, students can use activities from the "TOK Activity Interactive Feedback Exercise" to learn how to refine prompts:

  • Start with a broad prompt, then narrow down with follow-up questions.
  • Record the prompt history to understand how changes affect responses ("TOK Activity Interactive Feedback Exercise", p. 2).

3.1.3 Verification and Independent Research

After using AI to generate initial ideas or structure, it is critical that students verify the information with reliable academic sources. This practice not only enhances the credibility of their work but also reinforces the habit of critical evaluation—a cornerstone of IB education ("Artificial Intelligence Questions and Concerns", p. 3).

4. Ethical and Transparent Use of AI

4.1 Transparency and Acknowledgment

Transparency is essential when incorporating AI into academic work. If a student uses AI tools to generate ideas, structure content, or refine language, the AI's role must be clearly acknowledged. As noted, "if AI is used to generate ideas, refine writing, or provide translations, students must reference it transparently" (Artificial Intelligence Questions and Concerns, p. 2).

4.1.1 How to Acknowledge AI Use

Students should include a brief acknowledgment in a footnote or in the methodology section rather than listing AI as a traditional source. For example:

"This essay's initial brainstorming and structural outline were generated using OpenAI's Deep Research Tool. The ideas were subsequently refined and verified with independent research, and the final content is entirely my own work." — (Artificial Intelligence Questions and Concerns, p. 2)

This approach satisfies the requirement for transparency while ensuring that the core academic work reflects the student's own understanding.

4.2 Prohibited and Acceptable Uses

Prohibited Uses

  • Direct Copying: Submitting AI-generated content verbatim is considered plagiarism.
  • Unacknowledged Paraphrasing: Rewriting AI-generated content in one's own words without acknowledging the original AI input is also misconduct. As the "IB Academic Integrity Policy" warns, "rewriting AI-generated content without acknowledgment is misconduct" (IB Academic Integrity Policy, p. 4).

Acceptable Uses

  • Idea Generation & Brainstorming: It is acceptable to engage in a dialogue with AI for brainstorming, as long as ideas are then critically evaluated and re-articulated independently.
  • Language Refinement: Using AI tools (like DeepL for translation) to improve clarity or language, provided that such assistance is disclosed in a footnote.
  • Fact-Checking & Data Verification: AI may be used to quickly check facts; however, students should cross-reference this data with reliable academic sources.

5. Misconduct, Detection, and Consequences

5.1 What Constitutes Misconduct

According to the "IB Academic Integrity Policy," academic misconduct occurs when a student gains an unfair advantage by submitting work that is not authentically theirs (IB Academic Integrity Policy, p. 3). This includes:

  • Submitting complete AI-generated essays without modification or acknowledgment.
  • Paraphrasing AI-generated content without attribution.

5.2 How Work Is Submitted and Detected

5.2.1 Submission Process

Students are required to submit their work through official IB channels. Schools must ensure that the submitted work reflects genuine, individual effort ("IB Academic Integrity Policy", p. 3). This includes all forms of assessments—Internal Assessments, Extended Essays, Theory of Knowledge essays, and examinations.

5.2.2 Detection Methods

The IB and schools employ several methods to detect misuse of AI:

  • Teacher Verification: Instructors review drafts and compare them to final submissions. Sudden improvements in writing style or inconsistencies in the student's voice may raise suspicions (IB Academic Integrity Policy, p. 5).
  • Plagiarism & AI Detection Software: Tools like Turnitin are now incorporating AI-detection features to flag content that appears overly polished, generic, or inconsistent with a student's previous work (Artificial Intelligence Questions and Concerns, p. 3).
  • External IB Checks: The IB may conduct random sampling and investigations if AI misuse is suspected. This may involve interviews with students and review of working drafts (IB Academic Integrity Policy, p. 5).

5.3 Consequences of Misconduct

  • School-Level Sanctions: For minor infractions, a student may receive a warning, be required to resubmit the work, or receive a zero for the assignment.
  • IB-Level Sanctions: For serious or repeated offences, the IB may invalidate the student's work and, in extreme cases, disqualify the student from receiving the IB diploma. Appeals can be made, but the "balance of probabilities" method is used to assess the case (IB Academic Integrity Policy, p. 5).

6. Varying Use Cases and Practical Activities

6.1 Good Practice Examples

Brainstorming for an Extended Essay

  • Scenario: A student uses ChatGPT to generate a list of potential research questions about the environmental impact of technology.
  • Best Practice: The student then critically evaluates these questions, verifies them with academic sources, and drafts their own research question.
  • Acknowledgment: A footnote can state:
"Initial brainstorming ideas were generated using ChatGPT. The research question was developed and refined independently." — (Artificial Intelligence Questions and Concerns, p. 2)

Language Translation and Refinement

  • Scenario: A student uses DeepL to translate a passage from a foreign source.
  • Best Practice: The student reviews the translation, makes necessary adjustments, and includes a note in the methodology that DeepL was used solely for translation purposes.
  • Acknowledgment:
"Translation assistance provided by DeepL; final text verified against original source." — (Artificial Intelligence Questions and Concerns, p. 3)

Critical Analysis in TOK Activities

  • Scenario: During a TOK lesson, students use AI-generated chat transcripts (from ChatGPT) to examine how technology shapes knowledge.
  • Best Practice: Students discuss the strengths and weaknesses of the AI's responses in small groups, then write a commentary that integrates their insights with independent analysis.
  • Reference: This approach is illustrated in the "TOK Activity Interactive Feedback Exercise" and "Using AI-Generated Content as a TOK Object" documents (TOK Activity Interactive Feedback Exercise, p. 2; TOK Activity Using AI-Generated Content as a TOK Object, p. 3).

6.2 Unacceptable Practices

Direct Submission of AI-Generated Essays

  • Scenario: A student submits an essay that is entirely generated by AI without any modifications.
  • Consequence: This is a clear case of academic misconduct and is subject to severe penalties, including potential disqualification from the IB diploma (IB Academic Integrity Policy, p. 4).

Unacknowledged Paraphrasing

  • Scenario: A student paraphrases AI-generated content in their own words without acknowledging that the ideas were initially provided by an AI.
  • Consequence: Even if rewritten, this practice is still considered misconduct because it fails to provide proper attribution (IB Academic Integrity Policy, p. 4).

7. Practical Follow-Up Activities to Enhance Independent Learning

To move from AI-assisted research to producing authentic work, students are encouraged to engage in follow-up activities. These not only reinforce critical thinking but also ensure that the final submission is truly the student's own.

7.1 Challenging AI's Output

  • Activity: Ask follow-up questions such as, "What are the potential biases in this research?" and "What perspectives might be missing?"
  • Objective: This prompts students to critically analyse the AI's output, compare it with reliable academic sources, and identify any gaps.
  • Outcome: Write a short critique (200–300 words) that evaluates the objectivity of the AI-generated information.
  • Citation: (Artificial Intelligence Questions and Concerns, p. 2)

7.2 Integrating Real-World Case Studies

  • Activity: Request AI to suggest historical or contemporary examples related to your research topic.
  • Objective: Students then verify these examples independently using academic journals or reputable news sources.
  • Outcome: Write a mini-analysis (300 words) discussing how these case studies support or challenge the AI-generated ideas.
  • Citation: (Artificial Intelligence Questions and Concerns, p. 2)

7.3 Exploring Opposing Viewpoints

  • Activity: Use prompts such as "What are the strongest counterarguments to these ideas?" and then debate these viewpoints in class.
  • Objective: Develop a balanced essay that presents multiple sides of an argument.
  • Outcome: Draft a debate-style response (250–300 words) that includes both supporting and opposing arguments, ensuring all views are critically assessed.
  • Citation: (TOK Activity Interactive Feedback Exercise, p. 2)

7.4 Creating a Personal Connection

  • Activity: Reflect on how the research topic connects to current events or personal experiences.
  • Objective: This helps students develop a unique voice and ensures that the final work is personally meaningful.
  • Outcome: Compose a reflective piece (300 words) linking the research to real-world or personal contexts.
  • Citation: (TOK Activity Using AI-Generated Content as a TOK Object, p. 2)

7.5 Constructing a Question-Driven Outline

  • Activity: Generate 3–5 key questions that your essay needs to answer.
  • Objective: These questions form the backbone of the essay's structure and ensure that the argument is driven by independent inquiry rather than AI-generated structure.
  • Outcome: Create an outline where each section of the essay addresses one of the key questions, then draft a brief explanation of how each question relates to your overall argument.
  • Citation: (Artificial Intelligence Questions and Concerns, p. 2)

8. Ensuring Academic Integrity Throughout the Process

8.1 Transparent Acknowledgment

Every time AI is used as a tool for brainstorming, translation, or refining ideas, the student must acknowledge its role. This does not mean listing AI as a bibliographic source but including a clear statement in the methodology or as a footnote. For instance:

"Initial ideas and structural suggestions were generated using ChatGPT. All subsequent analysis, interpretation, and writing were conducted independently." — (Artificial Intelligence Questions and Concerns, p. 2)

8.2 Independent Verification

Students must cross-check any data or claims generated by AI against reliable academic sources. This practice not only validates the information but also reinforces the student's understanding and critical thinking skills. The IB Academic Integrity Policy emphasises that "authentic and genuine work is essential for fair assessment" (IB Academic Integrity Policy, p. 3).

8.3 Avoiding Over-Reliance

While AI can streamline the research process, over-reliance can undermine the development of critical skills such as writing, analysis, and independent thought. Students are advised to use AI as one of many tools in their research arsenal, not as a replacement for rigorous academic work.

9. Objectivity and AI in Knowledge Production

A broader perspective on using AI in academic work involves understanding the nature of objectivity. The document "Extent of Objectivity in Knowledge Production: A Cross-Disciplinary Analysis" discusses how different fields approach objectivity. In science, objectivity is pursued through reproducible experiments and peer review, while in history, it is a matter of interpreting fragmentary evidence with an awareness of bias. Similarly, in computer science, algorithms and AI are seen as objective only to the extent that they are free from human biases, though in practice, they often reflect the data and assumptions they were trained on (Extent of Objectivity in Knowledge Production, p. 1).

For IB students, this means:

  • Recognising that AI output is influenced by the data it was trained on.
  • Understanding that no source is entirely free from bias.
  • Engaging in reflective practices to identify and mitigate these biases in their own work.

By doing so, students not only uphold academic integrity but also develop a deeper, more nuanced understanding of knowledge production.

10. Conclusion: Using AI Responsibly in IB Coursework

In summary, AI offers tremendous potential as a research and brainstorming tool for IB students. However, its power must be harnessed with a commitment to transparency, critical evaluation, and independent thinking. The key takeaways for students are:

  • Understand What AI Is: Recognise the various AI models (ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude, Perplexity, Grok) and their functions. AI is a tool that simulates human thinking but is limited by its training data and inherent biases (Artificial Intelligence Policy, p. 2; Artificial Intelligence Questions and Concerns, p. 1).
  • Use AI as an Assistant: Employ AI for idea generation, fact-checking, and refining language, but always verify the information independently and ensure that the final work reflects personal insight and analysis (Artificial Intelligence Questions and Concerns, p. 3).
  • Acknowledge AI's Role: Whether used for brainstorming, translation, or refining ideas, AI assistance must be transparently acknowledged in footnotes or the methodology, not as a primary academic source (Artificial Intelligence Questions and Concerns, p. 2).
  • Avoid Misconduct: Directly copying AI-generated content or paraphrasing it without acknowledgment is strictly prohibited and subject to severe penalties, as outlined in the IB Academic Integrity Policy (IB Academic Integrity Policy, p. 4).
  • Engage in Follow-Up Activities: Enhance independent learning by challenging AI outputs, integrating real-world examples, exploring counterarguments, and creating personal connections to the topic. This approach not only ensures academic integrity but also cultivates a deeper understanding of the subject matter (TOK Activity Interactive Feedback Exercise, p. 2; TOK Activity Using AI-Generated Content as a TOK Object, p. 3).
  • Reflect on Objectivity: Understand that all knowledge—including AI-generated content—is subject to bias. Engaging critically with AI outputs and verifying them with established academic sources reinforces the principles of objectivity and integrity in knowledge production (Extent of Objectivity in Knowledge Production, p. 1).
